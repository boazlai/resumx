# Resume Screening Biases: What the Data Shows

When crafting a resume, it is natural to focus on formatting, keywords, and action verbs. However, a robust body of academic literature—specifically in labor economics and sociology—demonstrates that resume screening is subject to profound content-level biases.

These biases are consistently documented through "audit studies" (or correspondence studies), considered the gold standard in discrimination research. In these studies, researchers send thousands of fictitious, carefully controlled resumes to real job openings to isolate specific biases.

Here is a summary of what the data reveals regarding biases related to name, gender, institutional prestige, and employment gaps.

## Name, Race, and Gender Penalties

Names are the primary mechanism researchers use to signal race and gender in correspondence studies. The literature consistently shows significant penalties for non-white and female-sounding names.

### The "High-Achiever" Gender Penalty

Research indicates that while men benefit consistently from high academic performance, high-achieving women often face hiring penalties, particularly in STEM fields. A study titled _"The Mark of a Woman's Record"_ found that high-achieving male math majors were called back three times as often as their female counterparts. Employers sometimes viewed high-achieving women as potentially "overqualified" or "less committed."

### Algorithmic Screening Bias

These biases are not limited to human recruiters. A 2024 University of Washington study tested large language models (LLMs) used for resume screening. Using resumes identical except for the names, the AI tools favored white-associated names 85% of the time, female-associated names only 11% of the time, and never favored Black male-associated names over white male-associated names.

### The Academic Hiring Study

A massive field experiment, _"Discrimination in the Academy"_ (Milkman, Akinola, & Chugh), sent emails to 6,548 tenure-track professors across top U.S. universities. Using names signaling different races and genders, the study found that faculty ignored requests from women and minorities at significantly higher rates than identical requests from white males.

## Education and Institutional Prestige

Institutional prestige is a massive driver in hiring, but its benefits are not distributed equally, and non-traditional degrees face severe penalties.

### The Less-Selective MBA Experiment

A massive field experiment (Bennett, 2022) submitted 9,480 fictitious job applications to examine employer responses to MBA credentials. The study found that candidates listing an MBA from for-profit, online, and unranked regional universities **received positive employer responses at the exact same rate as candidates with only a bachelor's degree**. Even when the employer explicitly preferred a Master's degree, an MBA from these institutions provided zero statistical advantage over having no MBA at all.

### The For-Profit Bachelor's Penalty

A landmark study (Deming et al., 2016) in the _American Economic Review_ sent roughly 10,000 resumes to real job postings. They found that a business bachelor's degree from a for-profit online institution was **22% less likely to receive a callback** than a degree from a non-selective public institution.

### The "Online vs. In-Person" Penalty

Research isolating the medium of delivery also shows a stark contrast. A study published in the _ILR Review_ (Lennon, 2021) sent 1,891 applications, varying only whether the degree was earned online or in-person. Applicant profiles indicating a traditional, in-person degree received **nearly twice as many callbacks** as those with online degrees.

### The Intersection of Prestige and Race

Even elite credentials do not entirely erase racial bias. An audit study on college selectivity (_"Discrimination in the Credential Society"_) found that while an elite university credential improves callback rates generally, Black candidates who graduated from elite universities received employer response rates equivalent to white candidates who graduated from significantly less-selective universities.

## Employment Gaps and Unemployment

Employers heavily penalize resumes with gaps, often using them as a proxy for unobserved negative traits.

### Duration and The Caregiver Penalty

Research indicates that applicants with 52 weeks of unemployment face significantly lower callback rates than those with shorter gaps (gaps under 24 weeks trigger less penalty). Because women are disproportionately likely to take 6-to-18-month gaps for childcare, this "resume gap penalty" heavily skews against women.

### Formatting Mitigations

A 2022 study published in _Nature_ found that the format of the resume can drastically alter this bias. When applicants with gaps listed only the **years** they worked (e.g., "2018–2020") rather than the exact months (e.g., "March 2018 – October 2020"), callbacks increased by 15%. This formatting successfully masked the gap from the initial algorithmic or human cognitive screen.

## Conclusion and Methodological Note

These studies rely on the "correspondence audit" methodology, which measures _interview callback rates_ rather than final hiring outcomes. While critics note this limitation, economists argue that the initial screening phase is the most critical hurdle; if a resume is rejected at this stage, the applicant's actual skills cannot overcome the initial bias.

For job seekers, understanding these documented biases can inform strategic decisions about resume formatting—such as using year-only dates to minimize gap visibility or recognizing how certain credentials may be weighted by applicant tracking systems and human reviewers alike.
